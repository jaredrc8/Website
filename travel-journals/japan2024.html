Japan 2024 Travel Journal

Shinjuku City, Tokyo. April 26, 2024

Our flight from Vancouver landed at the Narita Airport at 3pm, Japan time. I had barely slept in 24 hours. Max, Khanh, and Cole waited for Chase's flight at the airport while I trained down to my hostel in Shinjuku, Tokyo. At first, I was very intimidated once I left the group. This will be the first time I have navigated a country alone, and the first time I really have no grasp of the language. I eventually figured out how to get on the Skyliner train to Tokyo. I had to buy a separate Skyliner ticket and then pay the IC card fee.

I hopped on the Skyliner that sped through Tokyo to Nippori station, where I switched to the normal subway train to Shinjuku City. My hostel, Tokyo House Inn, has two entrances for a part A and B. I accidentally went into the wrong one the first time, and then the next attempt found myself in a random cafe instead. I finally found my room at the hostel. I was bunked with a man and a young woman. Neither spoke English, so instead of embarrassing myself trying to communicate I dropped off my bags and headed out to explore Shinjuku.

My first stop was Don Quijote next door. Oh my god, they have everything. It's so stimulating in there with flashy signs and speakers on the shelves advertising their products. I walked all around Shinjuku and eventually to the Shinjuku Golden Gai, a neighbourhood of back alley bars. Very unique spot. I spotted a yellow Hellcat with underglow honking at an old lady crossing in front of him. I saw tattoos on the driver, maybe he was yakuza? Very interesting. Walking into Kabukicho, a big guy tried to ask me if I wanted a "sexy massage". There are 18+ signs and prompts everywhere in the red light district for that. I kept walking around, exploring. I found the Godzilla Head on top of the cinema and took a nice photo from the crosswalk from way back.

I also got ramen at Tenkaippan ramen in Kabukicho. It was cash only and I saw locals going in so I thought I'd try it out. I got Kottori ramen with seasoned egg and bamboo shoot. It was the best ramen I have ever had, so creamy and the egg was marinated and savoury. Kabukicho is so stimulating. There are lights and hundreds of people everywhere at all times. It is by far the busiest place I have been.

I returned to my hostel to write this journal and relax. The girl across the bunk from me ordered food and offered me some. I didn't understand at first, but she Google Translated it to say "do you want takoyaki?" I responded "hai" as politely as possible because I knew no other response. She split the takoyaki and chicken and fries for us and we talked a bit over the translate app. She is from Tokyo and 18 years old. I was confused as to why she is from Tokyo and staying in a hostel here but I didn't know how to ask. We finished our meal and I gave her thanks.

Shibuya City, Tokyo. April 27, 2024

First thing in the morning I went to Family Mart and got a salmon onigiri and a Boss cafe drink. I started my walk to the Meiji shrine around 6am. Around Shinjuku, there was lots of garbage on the streets from last night's partying. A few drunk people walked the empty streets and a couple of salarymen were passed out on the curb, sleeping with their faces in their laps. I hopped on the bus to the shrine and entered through the large wooden shrine gate. Trees crossed overtop of the large pathway, providing shade and cover from the light drizzle this morning.

At the shrine, you purify yourself with the water fountain outside. You are instructed to clean your hands, rinse your mouth, and then clean your hands again. As people enter the gates, they bow at the structure upon entering and leaving. Inside the shrine is a large courtyard with the temple structure on both sides. I went to the entrance and paid respects. Bow twice, clap twice, bow once. The doors are plated with gold and monks can be seen in long, white robes inside.

Next, I walked to the Shinjuku Gyoen National Garden, cutting through a quiet neighbourhood in the early morning. I was the first person there when it opened at 9am, and went to the Japanese Garden. The cherry blossoms were still slightly visible on the trees, but most had fallen to the ground by now. I walked all through the gardens and stopped at the Rakuu-Tei tea house.

I ordered a Sencha tea (green tea) and sat on a pillow on the floor. The tea was served to me on a low table and it was delicious. I overlooked the gardens as I drank it.

I trained back to the Meiji Jingu to see the Bugaku performance at 11am. This is an ancient imperial court and dance. The monks dressed in orange and colourful robes with a wavy-shaped headdress on. A group of them sat playing flutes and pipes while some others were dancing. It was difficult to see because of its elevated position and the public wasn't allowed to get close.

I then walked through Harajuku and Takeshita St to browse the vintage thrift stores. It was super busy here. I met with my friend, Masaya, who had been an international student at the University of Calgary with me when I was in my first year. We met up at the Hachiko statue outside of Shibuya station. It had been about 5 years since I last saw him. We exchanged hugs and I showed him the Kappa Sigma sweater I bought him. He was wearing his old Kappa Sigma shirt to greet me. We crossed the Shibuya Scramble crosswalk and he took me to his favourite ramen shop in Tokyo, Tsujita.

He ordered a fish broth with egg that was super creamy and the noodles were served separate from the broth. You dip the noodles in the broth and then eat it. It was even better than the one I had the night before! He also ordered a pork rice bowl, also very delicious.

We walked to the Pokemon Center and Omotesando Hills, the boujee shopping district. We chatted and caught up on each other's lives. He worked as an investment banker working 14 hour days, but now works at a MUFG fund that invests in renewable energy. It is a Mitsubishi bank. Mitsubishi apparently generates 10% of Japan's GDP and is involved in every industry, not just cars. He wants to do an MBA in California, and is going there to visit soon.

After exploring those areas, we headed to his favourite coffee shop, Streamers, and got some iced lattes. It was nice, reminiscing on the early uni days. He rented us a bike and electric scooter to explore the rest of Tokyo. Normally, you need a Japanese driver's license since scooters are only allowed to be ridden on the road, so this was a unique experience he gave me. We rode all throughout Tokyo to both Imperial palaces and around the beautiful loop of the main Imperial Palace.

It was interesting, sharing the road with cars in Tokyo. The traffic is very minimal and there are two sets of lights stacked on each other. The top lights dictate for everyone if it's a stop, slow or go and the bottom ones dictate if only specific directions are allowed. You can have a red light on top and a green arrow below pointing forward that means you can only go forwards. We visited Masaya's place of work in the main financial district, the MUFG building.

We rode to the Zozo-ji Temple next to the Tokyo Tower and as we were crossing the street we ran into Matthias, Cooper, Ava, and Riley of all people! We quickly exchanged hellos while crossing and returned our scooter and bike before meeting up with them in a brewery to say hi. What an insane coincidence, to find your friends in the largest city in the world!

After catching up, Masaya and I leave to go meet Khanh, Chase, Max, and Cole at Roppongi station. Matt and them were too tired to come with us. We ate at Coco for Japanese curry, although I was so full from the ramen I just had a beer. We hit Roppongi Hills and got pitchers of yuzu, a drink reminiscent of a gin and tonic. We had a few here and then moved to Masaya's favourite spot in Tokyo, Mogambo. It is more of a tourist bar, but that is why he likes it. We downed beer after beer (Kirin beer). We eventually hit the convenience store to get more beer near the end of the night and then got kebabs after our night out. We opted not to go clubbing since my legs were so sore from walking 38,000 steps today. I gave Masaya his KS sweater and we parted ways.

At the hostel, I met another Canadian named Alex who moved into my room. He lives in Iceland.

Asakusa, Ueno, & Akihabara, Tokyo. April 28, 2024

I bought a beef onigiri and an egg rice ball to start my day. Once again having woken up at like 4-5am, I could beat everyone to the shrines in the morning. I first trained to the Nezu shrine. It was dead quiet except for me and a few others. I purified myself in the water and paid respects, following the Japanese lady in front of me. The grounds were quiet and there was a huge flower garden and a torii to walk through.

I then trained over to the Senso-ji Temple and passed through Nakamine St on the way there. The shops were just opening up. Senso-ji is the largest and grandest temple I have seen so far. A giant red lantern with characters on it hangs from the centre of the gate to the grounds. On the left side there is a five-story pagoda and the main temple in front has a large staircase leading upwards. Inside, there is lots of gold found on the shrine and doors and structures. Everything else is coloured red. It was getting too busy to pay respects at the shrine so I left.

I walked down to the Kappabashi Dougu St to check out the knife shops just opening up. They were so beautiful and engraved in Japanese, but the nice ones were $400-500 and even the cheap ones were at least $100. I made my way over to the Ueno Park to meet up with Khanh, Chase, Max, and Cole. We visited and explored the Tokyo National Museum which held some interesting artifacts like religious masks depicting Buddhist deities and demons, swords, and armour. I left ahead of the group and got some street curry and ate it in the park.

I then made my way over to Akihabara, the anime district. It was so busy that they closed off the main roads in the district. All the buildings were sprawled with ads for anime. I walked around all the buildings for probably close to four hours. Khanh and that group had visited this cafe called maidreamin and recommended that I go. It was definitely a weird experience. All the waitresses are dressed in maid costumes and act very cute and anime. There were no photos allowed except for if you pay for it. I ordered the omurice and when it came the waitress drew a cute cat on it with ketchup. They also told me to do the "nyan cat" emote to get their attention (which I did not do). It cost ¥1000 for a photo with a maid (Khanh got one and it's legendary). The omurice was pretty bad though.

I left and went around Akihabara looking for specific shops next. I visited a few different card trading stores for Pokemon cards, a Jojo anime card, and a baseball card since baseball is so popular in Japan. I went to the animate store to look at the different manga and found an entire section of hentai on one of the shelves. I also went to the Taito Station game store and played their claw machines. For ¥200 per game, I got the Tanjiro Demon Slayer action figure for ¥600 (3 tries). I later saw it on a shelf for ¥5000 so I saved so much money there. A couple of European guys hyped me up and recorded me winning it.

I also got my girlfriend, Mikia, a Totoro stuffy at Radio Kaiku. I left Akihabara once my legs started getting sore from walking and went to the Imperial Palace to sit on a bench and relax. Once I rested, I walked around the grounds to the East Garden. By this time though, it was late and I only got to spend 30 mins there. Since it was getting dark soon I took the train to the Shibuya Scramble Crossing again outside of Shibuya station. I made my way to the Starbucks that overlooks the crossing. At night, the lights make it look incredible and it feels so alive with so many people. I also saw some really cool JDM cars at the crossing.

After killing some more time people-watching from above, I made my way to Omoide Yokocho with Khanh, Chase, Max, and Cole for dinner. Amy, Chase's girlfriend, and her friend Lena joined us in search for a spot. Omoide Yokocho is a network of back alleys with hole-in-the-wall bars to drink and eat food. We found a spot among the busy mess that housed us on their second floor. I got a lemon sour and some skewers (chicken, meatball, pork belly). It came with a weird white appetizer with little fish on it.

After eating, we went to play Pachinko, a gambling game similar to slots but it involves little metal balls. You turn a knob that shoots these balls onto a board with pins, like a pinball machine, and it somehow affects the three numbers that appear on screen in front. I couldn't understand the point of the balls and the staff had to use their translation apps to communicate with me so I never figured out how to cash out. I won some snacks though from ¥1000.

Tsukiji & Toyosu, Tokyo. April 29, 2024

Today's plan is to meet up again at the Tsukiji Fish Market for some sushi for breakfast. I arrived first, so I walked around and got some skewers from an unagi stand. I bought one unagi skewer, one scallop, and one unagi liver skewer. The liver was not appetizing to me but I wanted to try it so I still ate it. The actual unagi and grilled scallops were incredible. Then, I met up with Khanh and group and we went looking for uni: sea urchin. Eventually, we found a place that would let us share a bowl and got uni, ikura (fish egg), and tuna on rice. Alone, the uni was very fatty with a bold aftertaste and when joined with soy sauce it is elevated so much by the saltiness. The ikura was my favourite though. They popped in your mouth like boba pearls. It also came with tuna which was obviously delicious.

Then we went searching for sushi, specifically I wanted tuna. Blue fin tuna caught fresh. We found a nice sushi place to sit down and Cole and I shared a tuna platter with 8 pieces of fresh blue fin tuna. One of the pieces was a "rich" piece that had marbling. I ate it and it tasted like the fish was coated in butter! It was so incredible. The fresh tuna was the best I have ever had. After paying, we went and got cherry blossom mochi for dessert. All of this before 9:30am!

We walked and talked over to the Toyosu Market to see more fish restaurants. They had a knife store that Max and I went to that almost got me to buy a really nice one with a Japanese engraving and a long, wooden handle. The knives are extremely light compared to western knives because the blade does not extend all the way through the handle. Then we headed over to the Diver City Plaza, a 7 storey shopping mall with a giant Gundam robot outside. This robot was over 50 ft tall for sure! The mall had some cool items and stores like a One Piece store and a Studio Ghibli store. I got Mikia a Totoro tote bag because I remember on our trip to Vancouver she wanted a nicer tote bag from Chinatown.

After that, we went back to our hostels to rest and I napped for about an hour before heading to shop. I explored the Don Quijote and bought some snacks and cosmetics for Mikia. They had a free sample of soup outside. I told the guy "oishi desu" and his face lit up with excitement.

I went to Uniqlo to buy some pants. The fashion in Tokyo is beyond anything I have seen before. There's a lot of goth or kawaii clothing, outside of traditional or "normal" wear. Oversized pants are the rage among both genders. Girls either go goth with oversized pants or kawaii with cute skirts like in anime. Inspired, I bought some pants to hop on the trend. On the way back I found a random temple and stopped to pay respects.

I met with the group at Golden Gai and we went to Kabukicho for dinner. I was surprised that they had not seen it yet so I showed them around. We got soba and ramen for dinner. Then we went back to their hotel for beers and box sake. I returned back to Kabukicho and FaceTimed Mikia to show her around before going to bed.

Tokyo. April 30, 2024

I got up and hit the 7/11 by my hostel for a spicy pork rice bowl and a latte. I then made my way to the Yushukan Museum, and the Yasukini Shrine. This shrine is dedicated to those who died in service of Japan from 1869 until 1954, including several war criminals. This experience was very personal to me as my grandparents had lived under Japanese occupation in Malaysia, and the Japanese invasion of China is the reason they fled to Malaysia in the first place. I visited the shrine, being respectful on the grounds but I refused to pay respects to it.

The museum next to it features a Zero in the front lobby. Inside, the museum tells a decent amount of Japanese military history up until the 20th century. The story first shifts to the "Korean Problem". Japanese liberating Korea from Chinese influence, as opposed to expanding its empire. It also makes no mention of the comfort women.

It refers to the Second Sino-Japanese War as the "China Incident" and the Nanking Massacre as the "Nanking Incident". It says the Nanking Campaign was to occupy the capital to discourage resistance to the Japanese. And the Commander Tang Shengzhi ordered his troops to defend Nanking to the death and then fled, painting him as a coward. The Nanking Incident is described as the situation after the Japanese surrounded the city, they put up the Safety Zone and maintained strict military discipline. And that anyone caught committing unlawful acts would be severely punished, and the Chinese soldiers in civilian clothes were severely prosecuted. Those few sentences are the only mention of what happened.

Lastly, there is the description of their war with America. They cite that the US oil embargo triggered the war, not Pearl Harbour. They say that before Pearl Harbour, the White House discussed means to maneuver Japan into firing the first shot, and that Roosevelt ordered them to prepare for an attack, meaning Pearl Harbour was not a surprise attack. There are many more examples of revisionism, but those were the main ones that were just ridiculous.

I left the museum disgusted for having paid for a ticket. I decided to spend the rest of the day shopping. I went to Kappabashi Dougu St again to look for a nice knife. After lots of deliberation, I settled for a stainless steel nakiri knife from Tsubaya with a traditional wooden Japanese handle and a buffalo horn joining the blade to the wood. The nakiri resembles the Chinese cleaver and were used for chopping vegetables during Japan's predominantly Buddhist era. It transitioned to the kiritsuke for cutting fish, and eventually the santoku that is most common today.

Happy with my purchase, I took the train to Harajuku for more shopping dopamine. I got lunch at McDonald's and tried the roast beef soy sauce burger. I also got a bowl of spicy Korea fried chicken. I went shopping at Uniqlo again for shirts this time, and then went back to my hostel.

Tokyo. May 1, 2024

I had extended my time in Tokyo by a day from my original plan, but my hostel did not have room to accommodate so today I am switching hostels. Unknowingly, but pleasantly, I booked a capsule hostel for myself. The only thing separating me from the rest of the room was a curtain over the capsule.

It was super wet today, I had to buy an umbrella and my shoes got soaked all day. I headed to a tempura restaurant near my new hostel and got tempura and cold soba. The soba came with a soft-boiled egg that you whisk into the sauce and you dip each bite of noodles into said sauce. The whole thing was delicious.

I made my way to the stairs from the famous scene in the anime movie, Your Name. After snapping some photos, I decided to spend this rainy day indoors at some museums. I headed to the Nezu Museum, which featured artifacts and art from all over East Asia, including China, Japan, and Korea dating back to ancient times. Very cool to see how every culture develops similar artifacts like sculptures, paintings, vases, and decorates them with scenes or stories. It seems universal to humanity.

They also had a section on Peruvian textiles, which fondly reminded me of my previous trip last summer to South America. The back of the museum housed a garden with a tea house in the back and a pond with beautiful purple tulips. I went into the tea house and knelt down as I was served matcha tea and mochi with red bean filling. The workers there were wearing traditional robes. One of them was a white guy speaking perfect Japanese. The tea was freshly made and tasted like spring.

The next museum I saw was the National Art Center which was architecturally the coolest building I have ever seen. There were 3 floors of incredible paintings, all of modern art. Usually, I am a critic of modern art, especially abstract, after having seen the realism of the old painters of Europe. Even the impressionists I enjoyed thoroughly from that era. But abstract art seemed lazy, meaningless, and unskillful. This art has changed my mind though. I was astounded at the collection. I still hold those opinions on super abstract art with no distinguishable structures within it, but modern art can also be very creative in a way I hadn't seen here before.

I returned to my hostel at the end of the day and got Japanese curry with tonkatsu from Coco Ichibanya nearby. I got spice level 5 and it was very hot but I could still manage it fine.

Walking back from eating, I see a climbing gym across the street. After some deliberation, I returned to check it out. It was super confusing to figure out what the routes were because there were identified by tape instead of the colour of the rocks, and the wall was super dense so it was hard to make out where to go. Also, the grading scale was different and way harder than the V scales in Canada. A Japanese man tried talking to me. He asked where I was from and if I was studying here. kanada jin desu, I replied. He asked where in Canada and help up his hands saying Toronto with his right hand, and Vancouver with his left hand. I pointed in the mid-left between the two hands where Calgary would be. He was super nice and helped my find my footings on the wall a few times.

Fujiyoshida. May 2, 2024

Today I checked out of my hostel and lugged all my gear to Shinjuku Station. It was funny seeing people look at my giant backpack as they were all commuting to work. I found the Shinjuku Expressway Bus Terminal across the street from the main train station and waited. I bought an egg sandwich and rice balls covered in tofu skin for the bus ride to Mount Fuji.

The bus started slow with the traffic leaving Tokyo, but we quickly made pace with traffic clearing up as we got more rural. The mountains we approached were very small, but lush and green. Once I saw Mt Fuji though, I was astonished at its size. At 3776m above sea level, it towers over the little town of Fujiyoshida. The huge, snowy peak punctured through the layer of clouds below. I quickly checked into my hostel and made my way to the Arakurayama Sengen Park Observation Deck for a view. The view was perfect, and the small amount of clouds made Fuji very clear to see. It was super crowded, but I decided to hike further up and the crowd disappeared instantly.

On the way up this mountain, I met a couple of Germans also on the hike up; Aaron and Dion. Both were Muslim, 20 years old, and studying computer engineering. They lived just outside of Munich. We had a wonderful chat about our countries, our travels, and our interests. They took photos for me, and I returned the favour for them.

The view from the top of Shimoyama Mountain was no different for viewing Mt Fuji from the bottom, except now you could see all the surroundings and the whole town below. We made our way down together. They wanted to get pizza for dinner, unaccustomed to the Japanese palette. I opted to get conveyor belt sushi instead next to the onsen I planned to go to tonight. So, we exchanged information and parted ways. I walked to the sushi place and ate my heart out after that hike. The sushi was alright, but very cheap so I ate a lot.

I paid my bill and then went to the onsen across the street, Fuji-sensui. For only ¥1000, I got entry and a towel. Inside, everybody was completely naked. Men and women have their own onsens so it was only men walking around in this section. The onsen had a dry sauna, two hot indoor baths, one cold indoor bath, and one hot outdoor bath as well as a warm outdoor bathtub. On one side there was a row of cleaning stations. These were stations with stools where you sit and push a button that dispenses water through a faucet to a bucket. You can then use the shampoo or soap available to wash yourself before and after using the rest of the onsen. I cleaned myself from my hike and then made my way around the onsen, spending around 10 mins in each bath. When I would get too hot, I hopped in the cold bath. Overall, I stayed 50 mins. I got out and relaxed in the common area with a beer from the vending machine and a complimentary matcha tea.

Lake Kawaguchi. May 3, 2024

I awoke at 5:30am and raced to the same observation deck for some early morning photos of Mount Fuji. The sky was perfectly clear, and this time I noticed a few cherry blossom trees in bloom as well. After that, I returned to my hostel and took the train to Kawaguchiko Station. The ride offered a great view of the mountain. I stored my backpack in a shop across from the station and got breakfast at 7/11.

I spent the majority of the day walking around the entire lake. I walked from Kawaguchiko Station to Oishi Park and back. On the way, I heard music coming from over a hedge; it was a music museum! The Kawaguchiko Music Forest Museum was a European-style ground with concert halls and giant music boxes. The concert hall played a mechanical music box on the wall from 100 years ago in Belgium. There were little figures on the wall that sounded bells or drums to the tune of the music.

On the rest of my walk, I saw several instances of what I believe are cherry blossom trees. The view of Mount Fuji was amazing over the lake. There were people fishing, boating and even wake-boarding. Nice cars and bikers riding Harley's ripped around the lakeside. Oishi Park was super busy with tourists but had some beautiful blue flower gardens. I got lunch at a Japanese restaurant; tempura udon noodle soup. It was delicious as always.

On the long walk back, I stopped at a pastry shop for a slice of matcha cheesecake and a cup of coffee. Once I got back I realized I did everything I wanted to here and now I was tired with some time to kill. I found a sento (artificial onsen) next to Kawaguchiko Station and spent a decent amount of time there washing up and relaxing. The sento had a view of Mount Fuji and I had it all to myself since it was early in the day. It wasn't as nice as the first onsen though, it resembled more of a hot tub since it was a sento. I grabbed dinner at the 7/11 and then walked around the area looking for souvenirs. I then waited for my bus to arrive for Osaka...

But, Kofu Station is a 2 hour train away and has a transfer. Both train lines I need to take are on their last leg of the night. With this plan in mind, I booked the bus ticket departing from Kofu and hopped on the last train out of Kawaguchiko to Otsuki. When I arrive at Otsuki Station, I have 6 mins to transfer onto the last train departing for Kofu. Each train ride is an hour long, at which point I will arrive at Kofu Station at midnight, just in time for me to find a bench and get 7 hours of sleep hopefully. I can't miss these trains. Wish me luck!

Kofu/Osaka. May 4, 2024

Koyasan, Wakayama. May 5, 2024

I took several trains to reach Koyasan. It took about 2.5 hours on the train to get there so I got on around 6:20am and arrived at 9am. I met with Matthias, Cooper, Ava, and Riley at the Kongobuji Temple. Here, we purchased entry and learned about the lift of Kobo Daishi, Kukai, the monk who brought Buddhism to Japan from China during the Tang Dynasty. Koyasan was the first Buddhist monastery established in Japan in 816. It was said upon his return to Japan he searched for a site to conduct his teachings. The gods came in the form of a black and a white wolf and led him to Mount Koya.

Kongobuji was a beautiful glimpse into the inside of a real Buddhist temple. Each room had sliding doors painted with a scene from Kobo Daishi's journey or a Buddhist story in general. A few shrines were scattered throughout the temple which we paid respects to by bowing and meditating with our hands pressed together. There was a hall on the inside where a monk was preaching. Free green tea was offered, so we sat and listened even though we didn't understand. Red maple trees were scattered on the grounds. Out back, a rock garden in the rocky ground depicts two dragons emerging from the clouds (ground). Each rock is like a giant scale popping out and the head was a rock larger than the rest.

Next, we headed to the Danjo-Garan, a temple complex. Here, there were several temples and pagodas, and monks with shaved heads and long black robes walking about. They wore wood sandals that stood on two horizontal wood pieces. Some temples had a banner of several colours extended across the top of the front side. There was a giant, red pagoda and a giant wood pagoda. One large black temple stood out from the rest as being the largest. Inside, the largest gold Buddha I have ever seen and a shrine to him with many gold trinkets. Wood pillars throughout the room were painted with the Buddha in various positions. One pagoda had a wood turnstile around the base that you could spin. The purpose is unknown to me but I did it anyways.

We got food and coffee at the convenience store for lunch and then headed to the Okunoin Cemetery. This was definitely one of the coolest cemeteries I have ever been to, alongside Paris and Buenos Aires. The shrines and memorials to notable Buddhist figures and lost loved ones were beautiful. The trees in the area were so grand, the largest I have ever seen including in Canada. The cemetery is in the forest surrounded by these trees. The gravestones and shrines are old and covered in moss with kanji characters all over. Some had interesting legends behind them too. One memorial contained a well next to it and the legend says if you look into the well and do not see your reflection, you will die within three years.

At the end of the main pathway is the Mausoleum of Kobo Daishi. Inside is his tomb with shrines and trinkets laid out to him. The monks ran a table offering prayer ceremonies and amulets for longevity or conception of a child. I bought an amulet for protection, and one for Mikia as well. The monk prayed on it before handing it to me.

Outside the mausoleum were more temples, and a row of Buddhist statues with a box of wood pieces with Japanese written on it. A water reservoir with a ladle laid under each statue was used to throw water onto the statues. Rows of people were throwing water on the Buddhist statues and praying. The sign said it was for your lost loved ones. I too participated in the ritual.

The structure itself was nothing special when compared to the Buddhist and Shinto temples and shrines around Japan.

The Nijo Castle, next to the Imperial Palace was much more impressive in my opinion. This housed the Shogunate government for hundreds of years and was the site of the establishment and dissolution of the shogun rule over Japan. This one allowed us inside as well, where beautiful murals were painted on the walls and sliding doors of tigers that represent the power of the shogun.
